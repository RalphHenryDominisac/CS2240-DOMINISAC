Who was Alan Turing?



  An English mathematician, codebreaker, philosopher, and computer scientist, Alan Mathison Turing is best known for the "Turing Machine" which is his 1936 paper that defines the conceptual framework of a theoretical device which has the ability to manipulate certain symbols according to its rules.

  He is also widely known for the imitation game called the "Turing Test". in which this test is a certain approach of identifying whether a machine can have the capability of demonstrating human intelligence.



Early Life

 Alan Turing was born on June 23, 1912, in Maida Vale. During his childhood, he displayed signs of unique and high intelligence in which certain teachers from Sherborne School, where Turing studied, noticed it from him.





 When Turing turned 13 years old, he became more interested with the concepts of math and science. After complete his studies at Sherborne, Alan Turing enrolled in the university of Cambridge in order to study mathematics. Eventually, he was elected as a fellow upon graduation.





 In 1936, Turing delivered a paper which is entitled "On Computable Numbers, with an Application to the Entscheidungsproblem." This paper introduced the idea of a specific machine called Turing Machine (then eventually called as Modern Computer) that has the capability of computing any existing entity that can be computed.





 At a span of two years, Alan Turing studied mathematics with the professors at Princeton. He then received his PH.D and returned to Cambridge in 1938 where he took a temporary position to an organization called the Government Code and Cypher School.





 By the age of 24, Turing started to work in cracking the Enigma Code which is considered as a very difficult task to carry out. But in 1939, Turing broke the codes of the German Naval and the Air Force. Because of his astounding skill in code-breaking, he was then recruited as an Enigma Code breaker for the organization of Government Code and Cypher School, located at Bletchley Park.





 Turing also contributed in designing a machine named "Bombe." The contribution made it possible in breaking several German ciphers including ones that were utilized by Hitler's headquarters.







Career

Alan Turing performed as a codebreaker at Bletchley Park and took a big role in generating outstanding advances in cryptanalysis. He then published two papers that is inlined with breaking codes through mathematical approaches.





Subsequently, Turing started working for the National Physical Laboratory during the time of World War II. His contribution of designing the Automatic Computing Engine was considered as one of his most notable achievements.





Turing then became interested in the effects of technology on human behavior. In 1950, Turing proposed an experiment which is called the "Turing Test" that was considered as an imitation game. The objective of the said game is to analyze and observe whether or not a man-made computer can have the capability of having intelligence which comes to a point where it can fool a person into thinking that they are interacting with another person itself.







Personal Life




Engagement

Alan Turing and Joan Clarke were close friends ever since they met and worked at Bletchley Park. In terms of Turing's marriage proposal to Joan, it became impermanent due to Turing's characteristic of homosexuality.









Circumstances

Things got a bit complicated when Turing met Arnold Murray, a 19 year old boy. Turing invited this boy to lunch and interacted oddly with each other. Murray eventually told Turing that they were friends and possibly had a homosexual intimacy within each other.

Police investigations where conducted in terms of Turing and Murray's relationship together. Subsequently, Turing was then convicted to plead guilty by the advice of his own solicitor and his brother. In the 31st of March 1952, the case Regina v. Turing and Murray was brought to trial. Turing was then convicted and was given a choice between probation or imprisonment. The conviction resulted to numerous issues to Turing himself, this includes the removal of his security clearance and the prevention of working into cryptographic consults. Turning was not even allowed to enter the United States and was also prevented to discuss his work related aspects under the Official Secrets Act.





Death

Alan Turing died on June 8, 1954. He was found dead by his own housekeeper. Having a basis from the investigations, the determined cause of his death was cyanide poisoning. It was assumed that Turing committed suicide by eating a cyanide infused apple. But specific details surrounding his death remain a mystery.


















Awards and Achievements




In 1936, Alan Turing was awarded the Smith's Prize for his outstanding work on probability theory. The Smith's Prize is usually the name of each of two prizes that are annually awarded to two research students at the University of Cambridge. Specifically to students who specialize in theoretical physics and mathematics.











In 1994, Manchester, the city where Alan Turing worked until the end of his life, named a road in honor before him which was called "Alan Turing Way."












On June 23, 2001 , a memorial statue of Alan Turing sitting on a bench was unveiled in Manchester. The memorial statue contained a plaque that depicted a text which states "Father of Computer Science, Mathematician, Logician, Wartime Codebreaker, Victim of Prejudice" with a quote from Bertrand Russell saying "Mathematics is the science of pure thought.".














Time magazine, one of the most popular American magazine and news entity. It is a weekly news magazine and has gained worldwide fame for its content ever since it was founded in 1923. The magazine named Alan Turing as one of the “100 Most Important People of the 20th century.”














The £50 note from the Bank of England unveiled a new design for the note itself which contained a portrait of Alan Turing himself
